The team is selected based on a national exam that any upper secondary student can participate in. The next event is held in Iran, in 2018, and the team training began in early August, 2017. After the team had gained some laboratory experience in Aalto University laboratories, we gave them two presentations: a presentation about our project and another about Mathematical Modelling in Biology. The topics that we covered were history of computational biology, logic ports in the context of biological systems, ordinary differential equations and a case study of dynamically modelling differentiation of T-cells. We hope that our presentations were interesting and useful to the team and wish them the best luck in the Olympiad! Our presentation slides can be found from here. Turku Our last visit before the Giant Jamboree was at the University of Turku where we had organized an event 19.10.2017 with Eva-Mari Aro, Pauli Kallio and Eevi Rintamäki. We presented synthetic biology, iGEM and our own project as a lecture, where the main point was to get the students and teachers enthusiastic enough to form Turku’s own team in the future, since currently we are the only team in Finland. The event was promoted to bio students by Pauli Kallio and one of our sponsors, The Union of Professionals in Natural, Environmental and Forestry Sciences Loimu. We provided a flyer to help promoting. Since Turku city actually has two universities, there might also be a possibility that University of Turku might form a team with Åbo Academi. We shares some of the Biotechweek balloons, collaboration postcards and patches in the event and our banner was placed on a visible place outside the auditorium, so we hopefully reached a lot more people than just the listeners. In addition to organizing the event Pauli Kallio was also kind enough to give us an inspiring tour at their laboratory and telling us about their Cyanobacteria research. The Finnish Autumn was for once showing it’s best and all in all, we had an absolutely wonderful day in the beautiful city of Turku! It is to be mentioned that even if this was our last visit before before the Giant Jamboree, we will make several visits to the different departments and faculties of University of Helsinki and Aalto University as well as visit one high school and at least Thermo Fisher afterwards in order to spread the word about iGEM and synthetic biology even further. Kampuskarkelot Kampuskarkelot is an event organized by AYY - Aalto University’s student union. Its target audience is the freshers and new exchange students, and it is therefore organized in the main lobby of the university’s main building. Kampuskarkelot is a chance for student associations such as choirs, theatre clubs and events to market themselves to the new students. It was the first time that our iGEM association (Aalto-Helsinki Synthetic Biology ry) has participated the event. We got a chance to discuss with many interested students, who were eager to apply for next year’s team! Even students from the Aalto Business School expressed interest in the project - a good chance to make our team even more multidisciplinary! HEUREKA We attended the World Biotech Tour festival in collaboration with Finnish Science Centre Heureka. For two days in September we had educational activities in Heureka for school students and families. Our aim was to explain and teach about lab work and synthetic biology in an easy and funny way to get new generation of possible coming researchers and scientist interested about biology and chemistry. These events - Meet a Scientist Day and Family Festival Day where mentioned in the event calendars of both the Finnish Bioindustries and the European Biotech week. For explaining synthetic biology we created experimental Plasmid Puzzle which was targeted more for smaller children to challenge their imagination and possible give them a tiny hint what synthetic biology and DNA modification could mean. Puzzle was made out of colorful foam blocks that we made by ourselves. Every block presented some feature, like hot, cold, tasty, fluffy, sparkling, glowing in the dark, radio-active (or resistant for that), fledged and so on. Together five blocks created a shape of plasmid. We wanted to make attractive and really visual game for children to get their attention and we definitely succeeded with that. Kids from 1-12 were interested to play with the blocks and meanwhile we were able to explain principles of synthetic biology to them and to parents. We got really nice feedback about the activity from the Heureka staff. They said that this kind of activity is exactly what they are looking for and what they need in Heureka and they were willing to borrow the idea for future projects as well. In the end we also got possibility to be part of something very nice and valuable. After Biotech weekend our plasmid blocks were donated for a voluntary workshop meant for tortured refugee families coming from really hard conditions and especially for the children of the families. We were happy to be able to give our plasmids another life and purpose after Heureka. Play is the highest form of research. Albert Einstein For introducing real lab work and methods scientists use in their daily work we had an activity called Protein Hunt. It was a game based on simple color change reactions called colorimetric reactions is used in laboratories to find many different types of proteins in samples such as blood, saliva, sweat etc. We had 96-well plates with some of the wells including protein and some without protein. Every player had a chance to learn how to use automatic pipette and try to find hidden proteins from the well. We also helped Heureka to set up Pipetting by Numbers activity where visitors were again able to use pipette and create together an art piece. We created one platform for this activity and from Bio Festival came another we also took care of. It was fun to watch how patiently some of the visitors were coloring the pictures really neatly. Some of our team members were also enjoining a lot of this relaxing activity which was really close to mindfulness practices, if you had a stable hand! March For Science We attended a peaceful march - March For Science, in order to make the point that in this, cynically named, "post-factual" era, science is not highly valued. It is important to tell people that science is the way of acquiring knowledge - even if it is based on probabilities. The trending other opinion, trusting individual experiences, is far more likely to be dangerous for both the individual and our environment. Science is incredibly difficult to grasp, if you are not a scientist, and that is exactly where Public Outreach comes in. Let us help people understand science!
