    • Meet the team

    Our team comprises 13 students from TU Delft, Leiden University and Rotterdam University. We are a multi-national team with very diverse backgrounds, including biology, physics and industrial design. Thus, we are well equipped to tackle the broad range of challenges that will occur during our iGEM project!

    Click on a person or scroll through the profiles below!

    Tomek Diederen

    Function: Science Manager

    Nationality: Dutch

    Background: Biochemistry and Biotechnology

    February this year I was cleaned out from under my bridge, and heard that the TU Delft iGEM project offered shelter in a lab during the summer months. Turns out iGEM was a project about synthetic biology where I was allowed total freedom in the way I would like to conduct research! Unlike other lab-experiences, I could design experiments and then conduct these trying to prove outlandishly wild ideas and literally exploring the (redox)-potential of that bug that governs my gut-feeling: E.coli. Because of the large amount of team-members from other disciplines, and because of the duration of the project I learned a lot about working in a team. I learned the difficulty of communicating science to laymen, and the utter importance of doing so.

    Anne van der Meij

    Function: Science Manager

    Nationality: Dutch

    Background: Microbial Biotechnology

    The invisible world of microbes has always been fascinating to me. As a result I am a master student in Microbial Biotechnology. On my way discovering the little life I met synthetic biology. The creation of new biological systems is an exciting area of research and the iGEM competition is a major opportunity to perform this cutting edge research. For ELECTRACE I am mainly involved in experimental design and lab work. I am especially triggered by the challenges regarding the implementation of the extracellular electron transport pathway in E. coli. It is a great experience to do this with students who are all motivated to accomplish our goals and it is extremely exciting to match modelling results with lab results. It showed that the outcome of complex pathways can be determined by only a few parameters. This motivated me to take mathematical biology courses. The interdisciplinary character of iGEM is an incredible advantage for science!

    Sim Castle

    Function: Gadget building manager

    Nationality: English/Finnish

    Background: Industrial Design Engineering

    I am an industrial designer currently in my first year of master’s at TU Delft. Most designers haven’t even heard of synthetic biology (terrible, right!?), but ever since I first read an article about its potential a couple of years ago I have been excited by the future products it could bring. Synthetic biology is on the brink of being the technological revolution of the 21st century, and I want to be part of bringing that revolution to the masses! iGem is just the start...

    Mariëlle van Kooten

    Function: Science Manager

    Nationality: Dutch

    Background: Molecular and Cellular Biology

    Background I am currently enrolled in one of several MSc programmes Life Science and Technology at Leiden University.

    Interests Why join the iGEM 2014 TU Delft team? My interests are, amongst others, signal transduction mechanisms and signaling networks. These and other aspects lead to an interest in synthetic biology: to develop a minimal cell, understand the fundamentals of biology and begin the process of building a new cell and organism with optimized functions. To me, the complexity and opportunities in the field of molecular and cellular systems are amazingly challenging and the possibility of building biological systems and operating them in living cells is, in my opinion, grand. On a different level, I would like to explore approaching challenges multidisciplinary, creating a dynamic get-together in which it is possible to optimize existing strategies and consider new leads. I am keen to bring things to the next level in collaborations.

    Project Electrace Marked as fundamentally challenging, implementation of pathways for extracellular electron transport in Escherichia coli has been immensely motivating. (Modeling and) In-depth understanding of metabolic fluxes and improving and optimizing relevant (chemistry of) proteins continue to pose an incredible challenge.
