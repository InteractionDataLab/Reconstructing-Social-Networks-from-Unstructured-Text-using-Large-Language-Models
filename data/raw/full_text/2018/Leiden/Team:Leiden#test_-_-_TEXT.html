This way, our detection system could be used in Streptomyces overlays: experiments in which potential antibiotic producing strains are grown before seeding with a bacterial test strain. Compounds diffuse through the agar and - if lethal - prevent bacterial growth, creating a “zone of inhibition" around the Streptomyces. These experiments have many unknown parameters. Both compound and concentration are unknown in the case of bacterial overlays, making it difficult to test our chromoprotein strains initially. To assess whether the chromoproteins would be visible on agar after exposure to stress, a more controlled setup known as disk diffusion was chosen. Disk diffusion assays are used in antibiotic susceptibility testing and are simple experiments where instead of growing Streptomyces, disks containing antibiotics are placed on an agar layer with growing bacteria. Theoretical Model For interpretation of disk diffusion assays, a model-dependent analysis is used. Both the MIC value and the diffusion constant can be estimated from a series of experiments using a simple diffusion model. Traditionally, the diffusion of the compound is modelled as free, linear diffusion using Fick's second law: $$ \frac{\partial c}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 c}{\partial x^2} $$ From this equation, the following relation between diffusion constant ($ D $), MIC value and concentration can be derived: $$ \ln(\text{MIC}) = \ln(c) - \frac{x^2}{4Dt} $$ This approximation has been applied successfully to diffusion studies of a number of antibiotics, such as penicillin. However, certain hydrophobic or amphiphilic compounds cause zones of inhibitions that scale linearly rather than quadratic with concentration. Therefore Bonev et al. proposed a modification on Fick's law to account for this deviation. By adding a dissipative term describing the loss of antibiotic as it moves through the agar, the concentration over time becomes: $$ \frac{\partial c}{\partial t} = D\frac{\partial^2 c}{\partial x^2} + V\frac{\partial c}{\partial x}$$ This equation yields the following linear relationship between the diameter of the zone of inhibition and ln(c): $$ \ln(\text{MIC}) = \ln(c) - \frac{x^2}{4Dt} $$ Experimental Validation To evaluate which approximation was more suitable for developing a diffusion model, a series of disk diffusion assays was performed with nalidixic acid and E.coli Dh5α. Nalidixic acid concentrations were varied from 0.05 μg/mL to 50 μg/ml and the resulting size of the zone of inhibition was measured after 24 hours. Then, the diameter of this zone was compared to the natural logarithm of the concentration of nalidixic acid according to both models. Linear regression was used to determine the best linear fit (Figure 1). Figure 0 Calibration Fick's law. Calibration curve to determine the diffusion constant in Fick's Law.
