Microfluidic technology deals with the development and application of devices that control fluids on the microliter scale. These devices offer extremely high levels of precision over fluid flow regime, making them useful in biological procedures. These advantages stem primarily from the large surface area-to-volume ratio inherent in the size of these systems. These properties allow fluids to be controlled by pressure differentials and other viscous forces, and not the inertial forces present in normal scale systems. The other large advantage that microfluidics offer is the reagent input required is much smaller than bulk methods, which can maximize the material efficiency of a given protocol due to minimal reagent loss. The development of microfluidic devices is currently largely focused towards point-of-care diagnostics and organs-on-chip for drug development.
