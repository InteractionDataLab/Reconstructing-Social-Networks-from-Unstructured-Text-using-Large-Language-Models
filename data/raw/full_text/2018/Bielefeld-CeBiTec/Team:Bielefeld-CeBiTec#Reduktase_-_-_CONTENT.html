    Reduktase
    For the production of metallic nanoparticles it is necessary to find a way to reduce ions into the metallic state. This can be achieved by electrolysis or by addition of reduction equivalents to a reaction mixture. The most direct way would be a reductase which can confer electrons directly onto the ions. For this reason we try to mutate and tailor reductases to reduce the metal ions we want to reduce. For this project two different reductases are used to produce nanoparticles from metal ions. The first reductase, the mercury reductase MerA, reduces toxic Hg(II)‑ions into the less toxic metallic form. To work properly, the protein has to form homodimeric units. MerA has two subunits: the catalytic core and the ion catching N-terminal domain NmerA. The domains are joined through a flexible linker of about 25 amino acids. At the NmerA-domain, ions are caught by two cysteine residues. The catalytic core contains four cysteine residues, two forming disulfide bonds in oxidized state. Two of the cysteine residues form the active site, next to a flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) cofactor. The other two are located close to the C-terminus. It is assumed, that the C-terminal cysteine residues form an ion transport chain to pass the Hg(II)-ions bound to the NmerA cysteine residues on to the active site cysteines. MerA receives the electrons needed to reduce Hg(II) from nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH). The FAD cofactor reduces the active site cysteine residues, which in turn reduce the Hg(II)-ions. (Ledwige et al., 2005) MerA should be adapted to reduce different divalent heavy metal ions. Through site directed mutagenesis the metal catching domain should be adapted to recognize other metal ions. Since MerA has a relatively low reduction potential, it might be necessary to lower the energy needed for reducing specific ions by changing residues in the active site. (Ledwige et al., 2005) The second reductase is the nitrate reductase NiA from Fusarium oxysporum. F. oxysporum is frequently used for the biological production of nanoparticles. Gold, silver and cadmium sulfide nanoparticles have already been produced in vitro (Kumar et al., 2007). NiA reduces nitrate to nitrite but is also able to transfer electrons onto electron shuttles like 8-hydroxyquinoline. These electron shuttles are able to reduce metal ions like Ag(I),Au(I) and Au(III). The reduced metals aggregate to form nanoparticles. (Kumar et al., 2007) We attempt to express NiA in E. coli for the production of nanoparticles, to enable a recombinant expression in vivo.
