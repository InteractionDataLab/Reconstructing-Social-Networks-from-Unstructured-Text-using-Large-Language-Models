Our results show that using the flexible aminocaproic acid as this spacer makes resultant biotinylation more easily cleavable by biotinidase, and using the bulky valine stabilizes the biotinylation. Synthesis of these derivatives is straightforward, inexpensive, and proceeds in good to high yield. Importantly, the biotinylation reagents synthesized here are much less costly than commercial equivalents. Thus, we show that altering the chemical biotinylation reagent is a feasible approach toward influencing the stability of our cell mesh, and that designing and testing further spacers may help fine-tune the properties of this mesh . Introduction Purpose Any cell mesh produced based on our bioink will suffer from exposition to serum-containing media or, in the case of in vivo application, the patient’s body fluids because of biotinidase. This ubiquitous enzyme is able to cleave biotin off of lysine – at a lower rate also off of intact protein. We may now shift this hydrolysis in two directions: either to prevent it altogether and thus stabilize our cell mesh, or to accelerate it to make our mesh quickly disintegrable. To this end, we must consider how biotin is linked to the proteins in our bioink. NHS Esters as Amine Coupling Reagents N-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) esters belong to the group of amine-reactive active esters and are thus of great use in labeling proteins. Typically, NHS esters react with the primary amino group in the side chain of surficial lysine, forming a stable peptide bond (Figure 1). [1] The utility of NHS esters was first suggested by Anderson et al. [2] in the context of peptide synthesis; nowadays, a plethora of NHS esters are widely used in bioconjugate techniques and thus commercially available, including those of many fluorescent dyes as well as that of biotin, often referred to as biotin-NHS (see Figure 1). [3] NHS esters are typically synthesized from the corresponding carboxylic ester and NHS via the one-pot DCC coupling procedure [1] and purified by crystallization [2] [3] . Figure 1: Mechanism of the SN2t reaction between the active ester biotin-NHS and the primary amine of a surficial lysine residue of a protein to form a peptide bond. Being amine reactive, NHS esters undergo an SN2t reaction with primary or secondary amines to afford an amide bond as well as free NHS (Figure 2, left). While this aminolysis pathway is also used in chemical synthesis, e.g. of peptides, it is key for the above-mentioned protein-labeling capability of NHS esters. The latter, however, strictly requires aqueous reaction media, so that the undesirable hydrolysis, i.e. reaction with water to give the no longer reactive carboxylic acid (Figure 2, right), becomes a considerable side reaction even though reaction with amines, being more nucleophilic, is favored. At pH 7.0 and 0 °C, for instance, hydrolysis half-life is 4 to 5 h. For this reason, protein labeling must needs employ a great excess of NHS ester. [1] [2] [4] [5] Figure 2: Productive aminolysis and non-productive hydrolysis as competing reaction pathways in protein-labeling reactions with NHS esters in aqueous media. For applications routinely requiring derivatization of primary amines, NHS esters are advantageous for a number of reasons. Anderson et al. [2] describe that they are highly reactive and thus give good yields in amine couplings; moreover, being crystalline, they are easy to handle. Good yields persist even when conducting the coupling reaction in the presence of water, which sets NHS esters apart from several other active esters. [4] An important benefit of NHS esters is that, in their crystalline form, they are relatively resistant to hydrolysis and thus the concomitant inactivation, meaning that they can be stored for an extended period of time under dry conditions without special precautions while retaining their activity. [3] However, this comes with a caveat: solutions of NHS esters are susceptible to hydrolysis especially if prepared in N,N-dimethylformamide or dimethyl sulfoxide. These hygroscopic solvents tend to draw moisture, promoting hydrolysis of the NHS ester; such solutions should thus be prepared immediately before use. [6] In our work, NHS esters containing a biotin moiety, biotin-NHS among them, are used to biotinylate inanimate protein components of bioink or reservoir, for instance PAS-Lysine. For the printed mesh to be stable, this covalently attached biotin must not be cleaved. In this respect, however, we now encounter a challenge: our printing system involves live cells. These cells require cell culture media, which commonly contain serum – and serum contains the potential downfall to mesh stability: the enzyme biotinidase. How Stable is Biotin Coupled to Proteins? Biotinidase Hydrolytically Cleaves Biotin Off of Proteins Figure 3: Role of biotinidase in natural biotin chemistry. It serves to recycle biotin from degraded holocarboxylases. In the human body, biotin is a vital cofactor for several carboxylases, where it is attached to the ε-amino group of a lysine residue within the conserved sequence AMKM. [7] [8] To liberate this covalently attached biotin, biotinidase, or biotin-amide amidohydrolase (EC 3.5.1.12), naturally hydrolyzes biocytin (ε-N-biotinyllysine) to biotin (vitamin H) and lysine (Figure 3). [7] [9] The monomeric enzyme of size 76 kDa possesses a catalytic sulfhydryl group that is susceptible to inhibitors that compromise this functionality, including iodoacetamide, phenylmethane-sulfonyl fluoride, and hydroxy-mercuribenzoate. [7] [10] It not only serves to recycle biotin for renewed usage as well as to make protein-bound biotin in food bioavailable, [9] but also acts as a shuttle for biotin within the body [11] . Biotinidase deficiency thus causes multiple carboxylase deficiency, which, for one, entails serious developmental retardation because the body lacks the free biotin necessary to replenish its holocarboxylase pool; effective treatment is easily accomplished by administering daily doses of free biotin. [8] Biotinidase is ubiquitous throughout the human body. Its specific activity is highest in serum (5.80±0.89 nmol min–1 mL–1 [12] ), followed by other organs including the liver, where the enzyme is produced. [8] While biotinidase used to be considered fairly specific for biocytin, [9] evidence has accumulated that this is not the case [13] . Thus, it can also hydrolyze other biotin amide or ester conjugates, such as the methyl ester [14] or the conjugate with p-aminobenzoate, which forms the basis of a colorimetric activity assay [9] [10] . Biotinidase is thought to primarily act on biocytin or small biotin-bearing oligopeptides in vivo, since its activity rapidly diminishes with increasing peptide length; it is ultimately unable to cleave biotin off of holocarboxylases. [7] However, other studies have shown that the enzyme is capable of acting on intact proteins, such as biotinylated IgG [15] and histones [16] . Since biotin participates in the chemical reaction catalyzed by carboxylases and is thus somewhat shielded, this discrepancy likely stems from different exposure to the surrounding medium. The issue is this: With all biotin attached to lysine on both cell receptors and spacer molecules, we will invariably face slow hydrolysis if a cell mesh is exposed to serum-containing media or applied in vivo. Depending on the task at hand, we might desire our mesh to either be stable long-term or to be disintegrated more quickly. The bonding mode of the spacer’s biotin, which is introduced chemically, is key for driving this in either direction. Biotinidase-Resistant Biotin Conjugates If we are to prevent biotinidase from cleaving biotin off of biotinylated components in our bioprinting system and thus from disintegrating our mesh while simultaneously obviating use of special serum-free media, we must consider how to render the biotin bound to the spacer peptide non-hydrolyzable by biotinidase. Figure 4: Biotinidase-resistant biotin derivatives. Biotinidase-resistant biotin conjugates have been extensively studied in the context of the cancer treatment "pretargeting" approach. Here, biotinylated IgG is bound to the tumor followed by sequential treatment with streptavidin and radionuclide-containing biotin, which offers the advantages of fast accretion as well as clearance combined with low tissue retention. [15] [17] The general consensus is that steric hindrance at and adjacent to the peptide bond to be cleaved is paramount; for instance, p-aminobenzoate is cleaved off much faster than the m-isomer, and the o-isomer is not cleaved at all. Thus, resistance is acquired either if the amide nitrogen is tertiary, e.g. carrying an additional methyl group (as in biotinyl-sarcosine), or if the position next to this nitrogen carries a substituent. [18] Among the reported non-hydrolyzable biotin derivatives are those with the following substituents adjacent to the amide nitrogen: hydroxymethyl (serine), carboxylate (aspartate), ethyl (2-aminobutyric acid), isopropyl (valine), benzyl (phenylalanine) (Figure 4); a methyl group (alanine), however, is not sufficient. [13] [9] Since increased steric bulk diminishes the affinity of streptavidin toward the biotin, the 2-aminobutyric acid derivative has been preferred over e.g. the valine conjugate because it binds to streptavidin more strongly and is less lipophilic. However, this deviation may not ultimately be relevant given the inherent exceptionally high avidity of streptavidin toward biotin. [13] Inducing Faster Enzymatic Debiotinylation? To our knowledge, there have been no reports on how to improve the hydrolysis rate of biotinidase acting on intact proteins. We assume that the reason for the slowed or nil rate as opposed to biocytin is steric hindrance, which tallies with the prerequisites for biotinidase resistance outlined above. Thus, we must consider how to render the spacer peptide-bound biotin more accessible to the enzyme. Introducing an additional spacer molecule between biotin and the target peptide’s lysine residue should increase the distance of the hydrolyzable amide bond to the bulk peptide and thus facilitate hydrolysis. Based on the considerations outlined above, we synthesized three biotin NHS esters for covalent modification of target proteins: (+)-biotin NHS ester, the standard derivatizing reagent; (+)-biotinyl valine NHS ester, (+)-biotinyl threonine NHS ester, and (+)-biotinyl proline NHS ester, which should afford biotinidase-resistant biotinylation; and (+)-biotinyl aminocaproic acid NHS ester and (+)-biotinyl glycine NHS ester, which should afford biotinylation more susceptible to cleavage by biotinidase. The first were designed as non-hydrolyzable: biotinyl valine is known to be biotinidase resistant [13] ; since threonine is similar in steric bulk, we assumed that this derivative should be as well while increasing water solubility. The proline derivative was chosen because it combines an N substituent with steric hindrance α to this amide nitrogen, which should cause biotinidase resistance [13] . The second were conceptualized as an accelerator of biotinidase-mediated hydrolysis: Introducing a spacer between the protein and the peptide bond to be cleaved should entail increased exposure and thus facilitate cleavage. In emulating the natural biotinidase cleavage site, biotinyllysine-NHS immediately comes to mind; however, synthesizing this derivative is impossible without a protecting group on lysine’s α-nitrogen. To obviate protection and deprotection, aminocaproic acid, which is identical to lysine in structure except that it lacks the α-nitrogen, was chosen as the spacer moiety. While some sources cite this spacer as biotinidase resistant [9] , it has been shown to be hydrolyzed in in vivo applications [19] . Glycine was tested as well to assess whether even a small but flexible linker is sufficient for this effect. The assay was conceptualized for maximum simplicity and convenience (Figure 4). As is described in the literature for tests of biotinidase cleavage [13] [15] , we utilized human plasma as a reagent to obviate cumbersome isolation of the enzyme as described by Chauhan et al. [18] . Our model protein for debiotinylation was serum albumin coated to a 96-well plate and subsequently biotinylated chemically with biotin-NHS or a derivative. Debiotinylation was thus simply evaluated by incubating for a given time with human plasma, and the level of biotinylation was determined via adsorption of ExtrAvidin-Alkaline Phosphatase and the concomitant activity in converting p-nitrophenyl phosphate to p-nitrophenol, which can be detected spectrophotometrically. Calibrating the NHS Ester Concentrations for Biotinylation Since the level of biotinylation, which correlates with ExtrAvidin-AP activity, is evaluated via the kinetics of PNP being generated from PNPP, we have to ensure two aspects before starting the actual experiments: (a) the kinetic curve's slope should be within the dynamic range of the experimental setup, so that a decrease in biotinylation caused by biotinidase activity results in a maximal change in slope; and (b) it should be approximately equal for all three derivatives at time point i = 0 for better comparability. This was accomplished by assaying a concentration series of each biotinylation reagent in duplicates starting from 100 μM (BioNHS and BioCapNHS) or 1000 μM (BioValNHS) and diluting 1:2 in the next set of wells across one row (12 wells), and calculating the slope and its error for each set of two identical wells. This slope corresponds to the activity of ExtrAvidin-AP immobilized in the well and thus to the biotinylation level. Figure 6: Calibration of the biotinylation concentration. Left: Linear plot. Right: Semilogarithmic plot. With dA405/dt plotted against c (Figure 6 left), the initial slope of this curve is steepest before leveling out toward higher concentration. As such, the data appear to form a saturation curve, albeit less pronounced in the case of BioValNHS. In the semilogarithmic plot of dA405/dt against log c (Figure 6 right), the graphs of BioValNHS and BioNHS are apparently exponential; however, the slope of the BioCapNHS curve is sigmoidal around an approximately linear central range. This tallies with our expectations from a mathematical point of view: With rising NHS ester concentration, the biotinylation level will first rapidly increase and then gradually approach a horizontal asymptote signifying saturation, i.e. full biotinylation of BSA. The semi-logarithmic plot then transforms a saturation curve into a sigmoidal graph in which the central linear section is the target dynamic range. [20] We find both of these correlations to be true in our experimental setup, even though the sigmoidal shape is only achieved by BioCapNHS; the other derivatives have evidently not been tested at sufficient concentration to attain saturation. Interestingly, the development of the concentration series' data points differs significantly between the three derivatives: Compared to the standard BioNHS, BioCapNHS affords a steeper curve that reaches the same ExtrAvidin-AP activity at lower reagent concentrations and that appears to also achieve a higher absolute activity, i.e. a higher biotinylation level. Conversely, BioValNHS has a drastically diminished increase in ExtrAvidin-AP activity with rising reagent concentration, achieving a biotinylation equivalent to 1 μM of BioNHS and BioCapNHS only at 200 μM. This suggests that the biotinylation activity of the three derivatives is different, with BioCapNHS being most efficient and BioValNHS being distinctly less effective than BioNHS. Aiming to ensure an approximately equal initial slope in the following debiotinylation assay, we chose the concentrations 2 μM (BioCapNHS), 5 μM (BioNHS), and 1 mM (BioValNHS) for biotinylation of the test plate, the data points of which lie at dA405/dt ≈ 0.00035. BioNHS, BioCapNHS, and BioValNHS Are Cleaved at Different Speeds in the Debiotinylation Assay Figure 7: Results of the debiotinylation assay with BioNHS, BioCapNHS, and BioValNHS with exponential decay fit and the corresponding half-life. When plotting the initial slope starting from t = 1 min against time (Figure 7), we obtain descending curves in all three cases, suggesting that the biotinylation level decreases for all three derivatizations. (Note that data points below 1 min were left out, as they fluctuated strongly, suggesting that the system may require some equilibration time.) As such, none out of BioNHS, BioCapNHS, and BioValNHS appear to afford entirely biotinidase-resistent biotinylation of albumin. Nevertheless, visual inspection already shows that decrease in initial slope, and thus of biotinylation level, is not equally fast. To quantify this difference, each curve was fitted as an exponential decay to determine the half-life of the biotinylation level. We obtain t0.5 = 66, 2767, and 42  min for BioNHS, BioValNHS, and BioCapNHS, respectively. Thus, BioCapNHS is cleaved somewhat faster than BioNHS, whereas BioValNHS is significantly more stable against biotinidase exposure than either of these two derivatives. Control experiments incubating with plasma and PNPP without ExtrAvidin adsorption, only plasma incubation, only ExtrAvidin-AP adsorption, and only PNPP incubation all yielded a slope of virtually zero. For a practical example of how significant this distinction is, by the time BioValNHS biotinylation has been reduced to 50% its initial value, 41.9 or 65.9 half-lives have passed for BioNHS and BioCapNHS, respectively, corresponding to 2.44 · 10–11% or 1.45 · 10–18% residual biotinylation – i.e. virtually complete debiotinylation. Notably, the initial slope differs between the three derivatives, with BioCapNHS starting out highest followed by BioNHS and BioValNHS, indicating that the starting biotinylation level was not equal, even though the calibration was aiming to ensure just that. However, the incubation time with NHS reagent before the debiotinylation assay was 5 h instead of 1 h in the concentration series. As established in the latter, the biotinylation efficiency differs between the three derivatives. The concomitant difference in biotinylation rate explains why the significantly longer incubation time entailed non-equal initial slopes: We selected three concentrations to yield the same biotinylation level within 1 h; however, allowing more time entails that the biotinylation reaction can proceed further at the characteristic rates. The results of the concentration series have shown that BioCapNHS achieves higher absolute initial slopes than BioNHS, which in turn surpasses BioValNHS in this respect. Thus, the increased biotinylation time entails that the final biotinylation level will differ between the three derivatives. Discussion Syntheses: Performance and Mechanistic Considerations Synthesizing Biotin-NHS: General DCC Coupling Chemistry Synthesis of (+)-biotin N-hydroxysuccinimide ester (BioNHS) was adapted from Götz et al. [21] The reaction proceeds via a straightforward carbodiimide coupling procedure between (+)-biotin as the carboxylic acid and NHS, being an N-hydroxylamine, as the hydroxyl group-bearing component (Figure 8). Figure 8: Mechanism of the DCC-mediated coupling between the carboxylic acid (+)-biotin (1) and the N-hydroxylamine N-hydroxysuccinimide. The reaction involves two main steps: first, the acid’s carboxyl group conducts a nucleophilic attack on the carbodiimide carbon, resulting in an O-acylisourea intermediate after proton transfer; second, the coupling partner’s hydroxyl group in turn attacks the now activated carboxyl carbon in an SN2t reaction followed by proton transfer, yielding the desired NHS ester as well as the byproduct dicyclohexylurea, which is sparingly soluble in DMF and thus for the most part precipitates out of solution [21] [22] . As such, a large part of this byproduct is easily removed by filtering the reaction mixture before removing the solvent. Figure 9: Irreversible rearrangement of the desired, activated intermediate O-acylisourea to the non-reactive N-acylurea. DCC coupling reactions are reportedly frequently encumbered by the desired, activated intermediate O-acylisourea rearranging to the no longer reactive N-acylurea [22] (Figure 9), especially when using DMF as the solvent [23] . Nevertheless, this does not pose a problem in coupling reactions with NHS: the latter reacts with the O-acylurea so fast that rearrangement is effectively suppressed. [24] Incidentally, the Steglich esterification makes use of this same principle that adding a good nucleophile – 4-dimethylaminopyridine (DMAP) in the typical procedure – to DCC coupling reactions streamlines the reaction progress toward the desired product. [25] Boiling the crude solid in isopropanol serves to purify the target compound, as this solvent dissolves remaining dicyclohexylurea [22] and NHS [26] . Provided that the isopropanol is dry, the NHS ester is not compromised by this procedure, being reasonably stable toward alcohols; even methanolysis – with methanol being much less sterically demanding than isopropanol – requires several days at elevated temperatures to complete. [27] [28] BioNHS was obtained as a white, powdery solid in 90% yield and good purity (TLC, 1H NMR). Judging by the relative peak integrals of biotin and NHS moiety, the product contains some biotin, and the slightly elevated integral of the aliphatic peaks indicates that trace isopropanol may be left from trituration. Synthesizing N-Biotinyl Amino Acids: Mechanism of Aminolysis Adapted from Bellia et al. [29] and Wilbur et al. [13] , BioNHS served as the starting material for synthesizing the target NHS esters of biotin derivatives with different amino acids. The mechanism of peptide bond formation proceeds via an SN2t reaction at the active ester [5] with NHS as the leaving group (see Figure 1). While the reaction could proceed without added base triethylamine, this accelerates the reaction. [5] We must needs consider the efficiency of this coupling reaction, seeing as the amino acid is pre-dissolved in water. Under these conditions, the desired aminolysis and the non-productive hydrolysis become competing reactions, especially since both are faster in the presence of base. [5] Besselink et al. investigated the relative rates on N-Boc-protected amino acid NHS esters in a solvent mixture of DMSO and water, concluding that hydrolysis only becomes appreciable if water content exceeds 30%. [30] With a maximum of 3 mL water in 15 mL DMF, our experimental setup never surpassed 17%, so that the desired biotinyl amino acid is expected to be the major product. Coupling with aminocaproic acid is fairly straightforward, since the carboxylate group is much less nucleophilic than the amine and as such does not interfere with the reaction. Nevertheless, we should briefly consider the selectivity of coupling with threonine, which contains an additional hydroxyl group. Intuitively, we expect the amine to react preferentially, since it is more nucleophilic than the alcohol; indeed, as mentioned above, NHS esters react only slowly with alcohols. [27] [28] Thus, both coupling reactions should yield the desired biotinyl amino acid as the main product. Purification of these target compounds is easily achieved by washing the crude product with water, since they are not well soluble; as such, trace biotin derived from hydrolysis and NHS, the byproduct, are removed. [2] [31] Triethylamine is removed with the solvent while heating under high vacuum. Synthesis of BioVal followed a different procedure taken from the literature for this same compound [13] . In this procedure, the reaction is conducted in a solvent system of acetone–water 1:1, with sodium hydrogen carbonate acting as the base. We adapted the procedure slightly in adding 2 mL DMF to improve solubility of BioNHS. Notably, the crude solid is purified by dissolving in much water and acidifying to pH 2.0, at which point the vast majority of BioVal is present in its protonated form and thus only sparingly soluble; excess free valine, however, stays in solution as its amino group is protonated. Seeing as washing with water was impossible with the crude BioPro, this procedure may have been of use in purifying this water-soluble, sticky gum and should thus be kept in mind for renewed synthesis. Synthesizing N-Biotinyl Amino Acid NHS esters: The Targets Mechanistically, DCC-mediated coupling of the biotinyl amino acids with NHS proceeds in the same way as described above for synthesis of BioNHS. In terms of workup, trituration of BioCapNHS proceeded without any problems. However, purification of BioValNHS was encumbered by its unexpectedly good solubility in the recrystallization solvent isopropanol. While a precipitate formed when cooling to 4 °C, this dissolved at once when warming to room temperature, so that the solid residue could not be filtered. Since other amino acid derivatives are reportedly freed from contaminants by washing with diethyl ether, the desired product was precipitated out of the isopropanol solution by adding excess diethyl ether until large white flakes formed. These were isolated, washed with diethyl ether, and dried under high vacuum. The yield of these two derivatives was significantly less than the 90% achieved for BioNHS. BioCapNHS was afforded in 63% yield, BioValNHS in 41% yield. Apparently the recrystallization step entails more loss compared to BioNHS, which is certainly attributable to the product being better soluble in the case of BioValNHS and, likely, of BioCapNHS as well. Before conducting large-scale syntheses, these purification procedures should be further optimized by choosing a different recrystallization solvent or solvent mixture. Since BioProNHS and BioGlyNHS were impure even after recrystallizing, the yield of these compounds could not be determined reliably. As for BioValNHS and BioCapNHS, recrystallization from isopropanol may not have performed as desired, so that a different solvent should be considered. Synthesis of BioThrNHS was first thought to have been successful, seeing as TLC showed one main spot with a much fainter spot attributable to BioNHS. Nevertheless, it was puzzling that the NHS ester should have a lower Rf than the free carboxylic acid despite being less polar. When using this compound in a biotinidase cleavage assay, its concentration for biotinylation to achieve the same initial slope was 40-fold that of BioNHS; the assay then showed that biotin attached via the putative BioThrNHS was cleaved as fast as that attached via BioNHS. We conclude that the real active biotinylation component in "BioThrNHS" is, in fact, BioNHS as seen in TLC. This leads us to deduce that the main constituent was not, in fact, BioThrNHS. Taking the consistency of the product into account, BioThr to some extent likely polymerized during DCC coupling: the O-acylisourea formed in situ is reactive enough to accept nucleophilic attack by threonine's free hydroxyl group, so that an ester bond is formed between two BioThr molecules. This dimer now still possessing one hydroxyl and one carboxyl group, this reaction may repeat. We find an allusion to undesirable side reactions with biotinyl amino acid derivatives bearing an unprotected hydroxyl function in the work of Wilbur et al. [13] , who found that conducting follow-up reactions with biotinylserine at times entailed unwanted side reactions. Overall, the two derivatives BioCapNHS and BioValNHS were obtained successfully and in good purity suitable for the target assay. B: biotin (reference lane); BNHS: BioNHS; BVNHS: BioValNHS; BV: biotinylvaline. Thin layer chromatography (TLC) is a standard method in the chemical laboratory to determine product composition and purity. However, it is in many cases not sufficient to confidently assume that one's product is pure: for one, if one constituent happens to exhibit the same retention factor Rf as another in the solvent mixture used, we might wrongly assume that the product contains only one compound; for another, not all compounds stain equally with the standard Mostain employed for detection here, so that a side constituent might be mistaken for a minor impurity or even not recognized at all (Figure 10). In the present syntheses, for instance, the biotinyl amino acid derivatives stain very poorly, so that their presence and proportion are not reliably assessed in TLC. For this reason, we employed NMR spectroscopy to satisfactorily characterize our target compounds as well as evaluate their purity. 1H NMR Allows Us to Confidently Determine Purity and the Proportion of Minor Impurities, COSY Assists in Assigning Peaks Figure 11: Near perfect agreement of biotin (m, 3.10 ppm, 1H) and NHS (s, 2.81 ppm, 4H) peak integrals. Note that the NHS singlet is superimposed with another peak group equivalent to one biotin proton, so that the integral sum must be set to 5.00. x-axis in ppm. In 1H NMR, we can integrate distinct peak groups to find out their stoichiometric ratio within the compound. If a product is pure, we expect to only see peaks assignable to the target structure and the NMR solvent – and that these peaks have integer-value integrals if setting the integral of one peak to its corresponding number of protons. In BioNHS, for instance, setting the integral of one CH proton in the biotin moiety to 1.00 should entail that the integral of NHS's four equivalent protons equals 4.00, indicating that the compound contains biotin and NHS in stoichiometric amounts as expected. In reality, we must integrate the superimposition of one biotin proton with the NHS peak, so that ideally, an integral of 5.00 results (Figure 11). Subinteger values of either constituent would suggest that only part of the product is the target compound; for instance, if the NHS/biotin superimposition were to integrate to, for instance, 4.50 with reference to the above-mentioned biotin peak, we would infer that the compound contains excess free biotin. A further advantage is that the 1H spectrum allows us to approximate the proportion of an identified minor component via the ratio of its peak integrals to that of the major compound. Taking the above-mentioned example, the minor component would be free biotin. The NHS/biotin superimposition integral being 4.50, we would infer that the NHS singlet alone should integrate to 3.50 (minus one proton). Since this integral value corresponds to four protons, we would divide by four to obtain an integral of 0.875 per proton with respect to an integral of 1.00 per biotin proton. It would follow that the product comprises 87.5% BioNHS and 12.5% free biotin. In order to further characterize a target compound, a COSY (correlation spectroscopy) spectrum can aid in assigning distinct peak groups to the corresponding protons in the molecule. Briefly, this two-dimensional NMR measurements generally correlates protons separated by three bonds or less, i.e. geminal (attached to the same atom) and vicinal (attached to adjacent framework atoms) protons, and thus allows us to assign peaks in 1H NMR to specific protons with greater confidence. Interestingly, the two protons attached to the CH2 group next to the sulfur atom appear at different shifts, and COSY shows that they couple with each other. As such, they are not chemically equivalent. Even though in most cases geminal protons are equivalent, non-equivalency is commonly observed in conformationally fixed ring systems. Likewise, biotin’s favored conformation fixes one of these protons in the axial and the other in the equatorial position (Figure 12b), [32] and the permanently different electronic environments conferred by this positional distinction translate into different chemical shifts. The shift assignment to equatorial and axial proton was taken from Bates et al. [33] . Other correlations clearly seen in COSY are those between the protons of (a) the side chain’s first carbon and the ring carbon it is attached to; (b) the position α to the amide carbon and the adjacent aliphatic CH2 group; (c) the position α to the ester carbon and the adjacent aliphatic CH2 group; and (d) the amide nitrogen and the adjacent aliphatic CH2 group. Correlations along the aliphatic chains are difficult to pinpoint since aliphatic protons do not differ much in their electronic environment and thus form a broad multiplet of superimposed peak groups at low shifts. Moreover, correlations across the amide and ester group are not visible because they span more than three bonds. We observe three instances of unexpected coupling behavior: (a) the biotin nitrogen atoms’ protons yield singlets even though they possess vicinal protons. That they fail to couple is especially surprising since the amide nitrogen’s proton distinctly couples with the two vicinal protons (J = 5.7 Hz). This selfsame phenomenon has been addressed in the literature, without gleaning a satisfactory explanation [33] ; (b) the CH2 group next to the amide nitrogen appears as a quartet, even though it should couple with both the amide proton and the adjacent CH2 group to give a triplet of doublets (td) or doublet of triplets (dt), depending on the coupling constants to either of these vicinal protons. Here, we are likely dealing with a superimposition of these multiplicities making for a pseudo quartet; (c) both the equatorial and axial protons of CH2S give doublets even though they have two coupling partners: the vicinal CH proton as well as each other. However, the latter geminal coupling has J ≈ 1, so that it is not visible in the spectrum at the resolution provided by the 300 MHz spectrometer. Differences in Steric Bulk Cause Distinct Biotinylation Efficiencies The concentration series established that BioNHS, BioCapNHS, and BioValNHS differ markedly in their propensity to biotinylate albumin at the same reagent concentration. How do we resolve this distinction? It is unlikely that differing propensity to adsorb ExtrAvidin-AP is in play: while biotinylvaline has been reported to have a decreased affinity for avidin, this was in the context of a competition assay against regular biotin. [13] In the absence of extraneous biotin, the avidin conjugate should bind even to the biotinylvaline moieties in a virtually quantitative fashion. Thus, the only factors that remain are biotinylation rate and efficiency: if one derivative were to either react more slowly with the protein or be more susceptible to hydrolysis than another, the result would be that biotinylation level increases less with rising reagent concentration or that the saturation curve's horizontal asymptote is at a lower value, respectively. What differentiates the three derivatives that could cause such changes? The most blatant distinction is steric bulk. BioNHS and BioCapNHS are similar, but the elongated spacer arm of BioCapNHS perhaps makes for more flexibility and thus slightly decreased steric hindrance. However, BioValNHS is significantly more encumbered, since it bears an isopropyl group adjacent to the reactive ester carbon. Steric hindrance in NHS esters resulting in decreased coupling efficiency has been reported in the context of derivatizing a polymer containing primary amines with the NHS ester of 1,4,7,10-tetraazacyclododecane-1,4,7,10-tetraacetic acid (DOTA-NHS): at 50 °C in N,N-dimethylacetamide in the presence of ethyldiisopropylamine (DIPEA) and the nucleophilic catalyst 4-dimethylaminopyridine (DMAP), only 5% conversion was achieved within 16 h. [34] The authors argue that, apart from the carboxylate groups of ester and polymer electrostatically repulsing each other, the NHS ester's steric bulk is to blame for this low efficiency. While electrostatics should not apply to our system, the relatively bulky isopropyl group may entail this selfsame reduced coupling efficiency; moreover, the isopropyl group being a σ donor, it may somewhat increase electron density at the reactive carbon and thus render it less electrophilic. The hydrolysis rate may also be elevated relative to the aminolysis rate, seeing as water or hydroxide are much less bulky than a lysine residue attached to protein and thus perhaps suffer less from the increased hindrance around the reactive site. BioCapNHS Confers More Easily, and BioValNHS More Slowly Cleaved Biotinylation than BioNHS From the half-lives, we were able to establish that biotinylation introduced by BioCapNHS is cleaved at 1.57 times, and BioValNHS at 2%, the rate of that introduced by BioNHS. As such, we have attained our goal of developing one biotinylation reagent that makes our mesh dissolved more quickly and one that stabilizes it against biotinidase exposure. These results tally with previous studies on biotinidase activity and steric bulk around the cleavage site [7] [9] [13] . However, we did not find BioValNHS to be entirely biotinidase resistant in our assay, even though Wilbur et al. [13] report biotinylvaline to be exactly that. They assessed debiotinylation by attaching a radioiodinated moiety to the valine's carboxylate function, then incubating with a 1:10 dilution of human serum for 2 h at 37 °C before capturing biotin with streptavidin, so that cleavage of the biotin-valine bond would be expected to entail recovery of only part of the radioactivity. We cannot reliably assess whether the discrepancy of our results with those of Wilbur et al. stems from an assay design or methodical error in our experiment, or is in fact based on our assay using undiluted serum. Whatever is the case, we have successfully shown that chemical synthesis of biotin-NHS derivatives can effectively alter the stability of the thus introduced biotinylation and, by extension, of our cell mesh. Further designs and studies may be able to fully exploit this potential; for instance, introducing several aminocaproic acid moieties between biotin and the NHS ester group might make for a biotinylation reagent that affords super-fast cleavage, and conversely, choosing even more sterically hindered amino acids in place of valine may eventually reveal a fully resistant derivative. For each experiment, mean slope and slope error were calculated. Calculating Slope and Its Error We require a readout of the initial slope dA405/dt as well as its error. Errors were assumed solely as the standard deviation of identical experiments; errors of other parameters (e.g. time and concentration) were neglected. For a set of identical wells, the slope of the kinetic curve from 0 – 2 min was determined by linear regression for each well using the formula dA405/dt = [ n · ∑(ti A405,i) – ∑ti · ∑A405,i] · [n · ∑(ti2) – (∑ti)2]–1, where n is the number of data points and the sums include all values i = 0 through n. [37] We then calculate the arithmetic mean and the standard deviation of the identical wells' slopes to obtain the desired readout dA405/dt and its error, both in units of s–1.
